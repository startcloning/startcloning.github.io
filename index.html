Timeline

StartCloning

  • 1885

    First Demonstration of Artificial Embryo Twinning

    Scientist, Hans Adolf Edward Dreisch, discovered that it was possible to separate cells using a sea urchin. He shook two-celled sea urchin embryos in order to separate their cells which made the individual cells grow into two separate sea urchins. This proved that there is a separate genetic make-up for the embryos in each cell which can grow into its own individual organism.

  • 1952

    First Successful Nuclear Transfer

    Scientists Robert Briggs and Thomas King made a tadpole by transferring the nucleus of a tadpole embryo into a frog egg with the nucleus removed. Not many tadpole clones survived and the ones that did grew abnormally because cloning has less success when the nuclei is transferred from a more advanced embryo Nevertheless, this experiment proved that nuclear transfer is a possible method of cloning, and that the nucleus controls cell growth and development.

  • 1996

    Dolly The Sheep Is Created

    Scientists Ian Wilnut and Keith Campbell transferred nuclei from a cultured sheep cells grown in the laboratory into sheep egg cells without a nuclei (rather than the nuclei from early embryos) to produce lambs. They cloned a lamb from an adult sheep's somatic cell by transferring its nucleus into an egg with no nucleus. They named this lamb Dolly, whom was famous for being the first embryo carried to term by a surrogate mother, this further advanced stem cell research.

  • 1997

    First Primate Cloned

    Scientists Li Meng, John Ely, Richard Stouffer, and Don Wolf created the first primate by fusing early embryos with monkey egg cells which lacked nuclei. Next, they implanted the new embryos into surrogate mothers, which produced two monkeys, Neti and Ditto. This experiment showed scientists that monkeys can be cloned which are very similar to humans therefore, it is likely we would be able to clone humans if given the chance.

  • 2007

    Primate Embryonic Stem Cells Created by SCNT

    Scientist Shoukhrat Mitalipov and his colleagues fused an adult monkey cell with an egg cell without a nucleus, its embryonic stem cells can seperate to make any kind of cell. This proved that nuclear transfer in primates was possible and started the idea for human therapeutic cloning which can treat disease, this was a huge medical advancement.

  • 2013

    Human Embryonic Stem Cells Created by SCNT

    Scientist Shoukhrat Mitalipov and his colleagues produced a human embryo. The process they used is called somatic cell nuclear transfer and can be used to make embryonic stem cells. The stem cells made were specific to the patient they derived from.

What Is Cloning?

Definition

Cloning is when you take a living organism and replicate it to make genetically identical copies. A clone is an organism with the exact same genetic make-up as the individual it derived from.


Introduction

Scientists first discovered cloning in 1885 by using a simple sea urchin. Today they can clone things as small as a cell and as big as an entire organism such as an animal.

Along with the advantages cloning has given to the human race in medical advances, it also brings large ethical controversies related to religion and how the clone’s mentality will be affected.

These ethical issues are the reason we have not cloned a human yet, it is prohibited.

Reproductive Cloning

somatic cell nuclear transfer.

What Is It?

Somatic cells are any cells found within the body, excluding sperm and egg.

Somatic Cell Nuclear Transfer was the method used to produce the famous clone, Dolly The Sheep.

1. When doing a somatic cell nuclear transfer, scientists remove somatic cells from an animal they wish to clone.

2. Next, they remove the nucleus from the somatic cell and transfer it to an egg cell that has no nucleus.
This step is assisted by an electrical current that allows the membranes to fuse and become one cell.

3. Once fertilized, it develops into an embryo which is implanted into a surrogate mother.
The animal produced contains the exact same genetic material as the animal that donated the somatic cell.



The process of Somatic Nuclear Transfer




Abnormal Epigenome

Epigenetic tags allow the genes to switch on or off, which is what allows for the expression of specific traits and tells the cell to perform its given task.

Imprinted genes do not follow the normal rule of inheritance because both alleles are not equally expressed.
Unlike co-dominance or incomplete dominance, imprinted genes cause either the maternal or paternal gene to be expressed exclusively.

During normal reproduction, the offspring will receive one copy of the genes from its mother and one copy from its father. Imprinted genes are genes that stay in the organism for the duration of its life. Certain genes are “epigenetically silenced” because they only inherit one working copy from either parent.

Cloning animals through the process of SCNT can lead to many difficulties. When the nucleus is removed from the somatic cell, it already has different epigenetic tags.
The epigenetic tags in the nucleus do not match those found in the donor egg cell and instead of expressing one set of imprinted genes, the animal will express both. This leads to several health related problems for the cloned animal.



A nucleus being removed from a somatic cell



Applications of SCNT

The most common use for somatic cell nuclear transfer is in reproductive cloning. Dolly the Sheep was the first mammal born using the SCNT procedure in 1996. Scientists are also using SCNT to revive endangered or extinct species.

In 2009, scientists used the process of somatic cell nuclear transfer, in hopes of reviving an extinct mountain goat called the Bucardo. Scientists used a frozen tissue sample from this mountain goat which was a female; this meant they could only produce female mountain goats.

Unfortunately, even the longest-surviving mountain goat out of all the clones they had produced, died following birth.

Gene Cloning

genes and dna.

The Basics

Before being able to understand why scientists clone genes, we must have a basic understanding about genes.
A gene is a segment of DNA that codes for a specific trait.

DNA or Deoxyribonucleic Acid consists of the instructions for a living cell. In 1869, DNA was isolated by Friedrich Miescher but it was not until 1953 that James Watson and Francis Crick were able to determine the structure of DNA.

The structure of DNA is called a double helix. These men were able to determine this by using evidence from Rosalind Franklin’s Photo 51.

DNA consists of individual building blocks which are called nucleotides. Each nucleotide contains 3 parts; the phosphate group, the deoxyribose sugar and the nitrogenous base. The nitrogenous bases pair up by a method called complimentary base-pairing:
Adenine pairs with Thymine & Cytosine pairs with Guanine.

Each strand attaches itself to its complimentary strand using hydrogen bonding.




DNA Structure - The Double Helix

What Is It?

Gene Cloning is used to produce copies of genes or segments of DNA. Scientists often use gene cloning to make copies of genes that they wish to study.
The steps in gene cloning are:

1. Find an organism that contains the desired gene and isolate its DNA.

2. Using a restriction enzyme, the DNA is purified and fragmented.The restriction enzymes cut the DNA in a specific sequence. This produces fragments that match up on opposite ends.

3. The DNA fragments are incorporated into plasmids. These plasmids have matching ends to the DNA fragments.

4. The plasmid and the DNA line up, and using an enzyme called DNA ligase, it forms a bond.

5. Next, the plasmid is added into a bacterial host cell.

6. Now each cell contains different segments of DNA from the original organism.

7. The cells are then put onto an agar plate where they will form colonies.

Therapeutic Cloning

embryonic stem cells

What Is It?

Therapeutic cloning is the process of creating a cloned embryo with the sole purpose of producing embryonic stem cells. These stem cells have the same DNA as the donor cell.

Through therapeutic cloning, scientists can replace injured or diseased tissue. This also allows them to develop new treatments for diseases. Scientists can use stem cells to test the effects of drugs and to see if they cause birth defects or are toxic to humans.

Another benefit to therapeutic cloning is the ability to prevent rejection of foreign tissue because the new tissue is composed of the patients own genetic material.

Therapeutic cloning can play a large part in helping to save the lives of humans. Often times people who need organ transplants are not able to receive one in time or they cannot find a match. Being able to clone an organ for someone who is in desperate need is one of the many reasons why therapeutic cloning is so valuable.

Embryonic Stem Cell Transplant


Therapeutic Cloning For Parkinson's Disease

Scientists have used therapeutic cloning to help people who are suffering from Parkinson’s disease. Scientists have done various experiments utilizing a similar disease found in mice.

They inserted genetically modified stem cells into the dopamine neurons, which were then transplanted into the mice. The part of the mice’s brain lacked dopamine neurons similarly to humans with Parkinson’s disease.

Following this transplant, scientists observed that the mice showed significant improvements in mobility. This advancement allowed scientists to realize the potential of therapeutic cloning in humans. Nevertheless, therapeutic cloning must come a long way before changing the way we cure human diseases.

Benefits

why clone?


The Good

Cloning has opened so many new and amazing possibilities in the field of science.
Cloning is used in medicine, drug production, disease treatment, the reproduction of deceased pets, livestock and several other possibilities.


Medicine

Cloning can be used to find genes that cause diseases and illnesses. It can help with transplantation and regenerative medicine by creating identical cells which gives less risk to organ rejection.

Cloning organs allows for the creation of an organ that is perfectly suited to the patient. Not only does this help to simplify the surgical process but it also helps patients once they have left the hospital.

Many transplant patients must take an immunosuppressant for years or even the rest of their lives in order to prevent organ rejection. Immunosuppressant drugs such as Cyclosporine typically cause unpleasant side effects and increase the patient’s susceptibility to infections. However, if the organ they receive through cloning is genetically identical, then the rejection risk is much lower.


Cloning Deceased Pets

The cloning of deceased pets has become more and more popular over the past few years.
Although there is a very high price tag, pet owners around the world are ready to do whatever it takes to keep their furry friends alive.

One very important fact to note is that these cloned animals are not exactly like the donor of its genetic material. Many different factors play a role in the non-identical clones such as the environment. This is because of their epigenetics which are the heritable changes in gene expression. This is not caused by the actual DNA sequence of the animal.

The first ever cloned cat, Rainbow (right) and her clone, Carbon Copy (left)



Genetic Fingerprinting


Cloning can help the police identify criminals through genetic fingerprinting. They take the DNA from an individual’s blood and cut it with restriction enzymes.

Everyone has different and unique pieces of DNA that’s been cut and therefore the police can match the killer’s blood, saliva or any bodily fluid to the sample they examine.



Mass Production Of Livestock and Crop Plants

Cloning can make plants resistant to pest damage and diseases, causing them to be better quality, grow faster, and produce more food for us.

Cloning technologies have allowed for the reproduction of prized animals and plants on a large scale. This enables them to increase crop yields and produce the best quality products for the market.

The most commonly cloned farm animals are cattle, pigs, sheep and goat.

Controversies

stop cloning?

The Bad

Along with all the great benefits that cloning has to offer, there are even more controversies to discuss. Not only does cloning raise several concerns regarding human rights, there is also the religious aspect to consider as well.

Ever since cloning was introduced to society, humans have come to realize the issues that this technology will bring. It’s a question of what the appropriate cost is for medical advancements and the possibility of being able to clone ourselves.

The ethical debate over cloning humans has come to a standstill due to the overall consensus that the cloning of humans is just too dangerous.


Failure Rate

Cloning has a high rate of failure. In the case of humans, the largest debate is whether or not the death of a clone is considered murder.

Tests on cloning animals have shown scientists that the rate of success is approximately 3%, meaning that 97% of human clones could potentially suffer from serious physical or mental disabilities, if they even make it into the world.

Cloned animals have shown various developmental problems such as kidney defects, missing organs and being prone to infections. A cloned animal’s lifespan is significantly less than that of a regular human. These clones become very ill and are often euthanized early on in their lives.


Religion & Culture

Religion and culture are a large factor when considering cloning because it goes against many people’s beliefs about human life.

Many believe that the ability to clone any human gives less purpose to one’s life, successes, discoveries and intelligence. Clones may be judged by their own community because people who know their origin, may see them as lesser of a human than those who were conceived naturally.

Many Christians believe that life is given by God and therefore, humans should not have the power to replicate someone who was specifically made as an individual from a higher power.


Change in Society & The Family Dynamic

In the arguments for cloning, some may say that if cloning can be done safely on animals, then it should be perfectly fine to do cloning for humans. The problem with this statement is that animals cannot express how they feel about cloning.

Clones would have a lack of freedom and unique genetic identity. This could give rise to several health problems, both mental and physical. Cloning takes away the individuality of a human and can also lead to a future problem in terms of genetic diversity. If people begin cloning their children instead of relying on natural fertilization, then there would not be a vast difference in the genetics of a population.

In a family setting, the clones might not meet the expectations of their parents, who have essentially decided their genetic makeup. If a couple is facing infertility and they decide to clone the mother, genetically speaking, their “daughter” would actually be the mother’s twin.
This results in a very complicated family dynamic and changes the structure and nature of the family.
